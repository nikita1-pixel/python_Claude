### Concept 1: INNER JOIN
> Why it exists: real data is split across many tables (customers in one, orders in another). A JOIN glues them back together on a shared key so you can answer "who bought what".

The setup: two tiny tables.
- `customers` — WHO they are (customer_id, name, city)
- `orders` — WHAT they bought (order_id, customer_id, product, amount)

The link between them is **customer_id** — it appears in both tables. That shared column is called the **key**. An order knows *which* customer placed it only by storing that customer's id.

An **INNER JOIN** says: "give me rows where the key matches in BOTH tables." The syntax:

```sql
FROM orders
INNER JOIN customers
    ON orders.customer_id = customers.customer_id
```

- `FROM orders` — start with the orders table
- `INNER JOIN customers` — bring in the customers table
- `ON orders.customer_id = customers.customer_id` — the rule for matching rows: glue an order to a customer when their customer_ids are equal
- Because a column name like `customer_id` lives in both tables, we write `table.column` (e.g. `orders.amount`, `customers.name`) so SQL knows which one we mean.

Two deliberate "trap" rows in the data:
- **Sneha** (customer 4) exists but placed NO orders.
- Order 105 has **customer_id 9**, which matches NO customer.

INNER JOIN drops BOTH of those — no match on either side means the row disappears. That's the whole personality of INNER: matches only.

**Ran it — output:** INNER JOIN returned **4 rows** (Aarav ×2, Priya, Rohan). Webcam (customer_id 9) and Sneha (no orders) both dropped — no match.
**Predict-and-change:** Nikita predicted 4 before running. Correct.
**Sanity check:** ✓ Understood that INNER keeps only matched rows on both sides.

### Concept 2: LEFT JOIN
> Why it exists: sometimes you want ALL rows from your main table even when there's no match — e.g. "list every customer AND their orders, including customers who never bought anything."

Nikita wrote the LEFT JOIN herself (filled all 4 blanks correctly):
`SELECT customers.name, customers.city, orders.product, orders.amount FROM customers LEFT JOIN orders ON customers.customer_id = orders.customer_id`

**Ran it — output:** 5 rows. Sneha now appears with NaN product/amount (NULL = nothing to attach). Webcam (right-side orphan, customer_id 9) still dropped — LEFT only protects the left table.
**Sanity check:** ✓ Understood direction matters: left table is protected, right-side orphans still vanish. NaN = SQL NULL in Pandas.

### Concept 3: Aggregating across a JOIN
> Why it exists: the real payoff — once two tables are glued, you can answer business questions like "how much has each customer spent in total?"

Nikita wrote the query herself. Hit two bugs and **debugged them by reading the error** (`near ")": syntax error`):
- Bug 1: `ROUND(SUM(orders.amount), 2 ))` — one extra `)`. Taught paren-counting (every `(` needs one `)`).
- Bug 2: `GROUPBY` → must be two words `GROUP BY`.

Final working query: `SELECT customers.name, ROUND(SUM(orders.amount), 2) AS total_spent FROM customers LEFT JOIN orders ON customers.customer_id = orders.customer_id GROUP BY customers.name ORDER BY total_spent DESC`

**Ran it — output:** Rohan 55000, Priya 9000, Aarav 1700 (1200+500 grouped & summed), Sneha NaN.
**Sanity check:** ✓ Aarav prediction correct. Key insight: SUM of no rows = NULL/NaN, NOT 0 ("no data" ≠ "zero"). Bonus taught: `COALESCE(SUM(...), 0)` to show 0 instead of blank.
